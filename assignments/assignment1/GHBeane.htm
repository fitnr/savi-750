Map Analysis

“U.S. Reaches Goal of Admitting 10,000 Syrian Refugees. Here’s Where They Went.” New York Times. August, 2016.

 

This map describes refugee placement since 2012, mapped over existing refugee population (per 100,000 residents). It accompanies an article from the NYT that describes how and where Syrian refugees have been settled since the beginning of the Syrian civil war. While there are some important pieces of information lost in the map – for instance, the size of the new refugee populations, which is indicated only by “town or city where at least one refugee placed since 2012” – the message is imparted in a clear and elegant manner. Typically, the Times is effective at not over-saturating their maps with information; by controlling the number of variables displayed, in part by introducing this map as one in a series of images, they effectively limit graphic “noise.”

 

What is striking about the map is that in some ways it actually refutes, or at least nuances the Times’ argument that Syrian refugees are settled, when possible, near existing Syrian populations to ease integration.  In fact, there appear to be large Syrian populations in the Midwestern states that have received no refugees. The possible reasons for this are explored in subsequent maps.
